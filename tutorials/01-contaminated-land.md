# 1: Contaminated Land and the Part 2A Regime

> **Educational use only.** This tutorial summarises UK law for study purposes. It is not legal advice and the authors accept no liability.

## What is "contaminated land"?

In England, "contaminated land" is a technical term defined by **Part 2A of the Environmental Protection Act 1990** (as amended). Land is *statutorily* contaminated if there is a **significant possibility of significant harm (SPOSH)** being caused by substances in, on, or under the land — or if there is a significant possibility of significant pollution of controlled waters.

Radioactive contamination was brought into scope by:

- Environment Act 1995, s. 57
- The **Radioactive Contaminated Land (Enabling Powers) (England) Regulations 2005** (SI 2005/3467) — and their 2010 and 2018 amendments
- Council Directive **2013/59/Euratom** (the 2013 Basic Safety Standards Directive)

Put together, these mean that contaminated land caused by **radioactivity** is handled under the same statutory framework as chemically contaminated land, but with its own thresholds and methodology — RCLEA.

## The three harm thresholds

Under the statutory guidance, land is considered to cause significant harm if lasting exposure from the contaminant linkage would result in effective doses exceeding **any** of:

| Dose type | Threshold |
|---|---|
| **Effective dose** (whole body) | **3 mSv / year** |
| Equivalent dose to the lens of the eye | 15 mSv / year |
| Equivalent dose to skin | 50 mSv / year |

The 3 mSv/y effective-dose threshold is the one RCLEA's "total effective dose" is compared against. Background radiation is **excluded** — the assessment only counts the anthropogenic contamination.

## Who does what

| Body | Role |
|---|---|
| **Local Authority** | Duty to inspect land, determine whether it is contaminated, serve remediation notices (lead enforcer for chemical contamination). |
| **Environment Agency** | Enforcing authority specifically for radioactive contaminated land. Advises on methodology. May recover remediation costs. |
| **UKHSA** (formerly PHE) | Public-health advisor. Authored the technical principles (CRCE-RAD-003-2020). |
| **Secretary of State** | Liable for contamination from "nuclear occurrences"; issues statutory guidance. |

## The decision logic (simplified)

```
For each possible contaminant linkage (source → pathway → receptor):
    1. Can lasting exposure cause an effective dose > 3 mSv/y?     ← HARM
    2. If yes, is there a significant possibility of that occurring? ← POSSIBILITY
    3. Can existing risk-management measures prevent it?             ← MITIGATION
    → If (1 & 2) and not (3), the land is "contaminated land"
```

RCLEA addresses step 1 — the dose calculation — in a conservative, screening-tier form.

## Reflection

Before moving on, try this:

> Imagine you are a local authority officer reviewing a site used as a 1960s luminiser factory. Radium-226 is present in backfill. What are the *three* things you would want to know before running an RCLEA assessment?

A good answer would include: (a) the **measured soil concentration** and spatial extent, (b) the **intended land use** (residential? industrial? public park?), (c) the **receptor** most at risk (are there children? indoor occupancy?). RCLEA's output depends on all three.
